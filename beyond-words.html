Beyond Words

Say "I'm fine" three ways in your head. Flat. Fast, cutting the conversation short. Slow, with a small pause before it lands.

Same words. Completely different information. None of that information came from the dictionary definition of I'm or fine. It came from somewhere else entirely.

Linguists have a name for part of that second channel: metadiscourse — everything in language that isn't about the topic itself, but about how the speaker is relating to it. Ken Hyland mapped this in remarkable detail: how certain someone sounds (might versus clearly), how present they are in what they're saying (I found... versus it was found...), how much they're inviting the listener in, how strongly they're committing to a claim. None of it changes the facts being reported. All of it changes how those facts are experienced.

Gestalt therapy has long paid attention to a closely related phenomenon. Form and content aren't separate. The way something is said is itself meaningful. Meaning doesn't live only in the semantic content of language; it also lives in rhythm, hesitation, emphasis, ownership, and relationship.

Knowing what someone said is only part of listening.

Almost none of this happens under conscious control. Nobody sits down thinking, I'll hedge this sentence a little, or I'll boost this claim to sound more convincing. These patterns emerge from habit, experience, relationship, uncertainty, confidence, and history. In that sense, they are often one of the more honest channels available — harder to deliberately perform than the content riding on top of them.

You begin noticing this after you've listened to someone for a while. Every person develops a linguistic fingerprint: favorite words, familiar rhythms, even preferred sentence shapes. Then, one day, something appears that doesn't quite belong.

Maybe a friend who almost always says I think suddenly says Obviously...

Someone whose language is consistently concrete suddenly describes an experience as transformative.

Or imagine someone who always orders coffee saying, I'll have a beverage.

Nothing is objectively wrong with any of these sentences. Yet most of us immediately feel that something has shifted. Not because the facts changed, but because the language no longer fits the speaker we thought we knew.

Sometimes that's all it is: a phrase picked up from a podcast, a colleague, yesterday's conversation. But sometimes it's more interesting. An idea that hasn't quite become one's own yet. A sentence spoken partly in someone else's voice.

Gestalt therapy has a word for this: introjection — taking something in before it has been fully digested and integrated into one's own way of experiencing the world.

The interesting question isn't whether the borrowed word is "correct." It's what work it's doing. Does it fit naturally with everything around it? Does the rest of the person's language reorganize itself around this new concept? Or does it remain like a borrowed stone in an otherwise familiar wall — drawing attention precisely because it doesn't quite settle?

This is where I find an unexpected bridge to natural language processing. Words are not isolated symbols. Every word shifts the landscape of possibilities for what comes next, subtly reshaping the meaning of the words around it. One unexpected word can change our expectations about the speaker, the emotional tone, the relationship, even the conversation we're now anticipating. We rarely notice ourselves doing it, but we do it continuously.

Listening this way is about developing sensitivity to another channel of information. The content tells us what someone is saying. The texture tells us something about how that meaning currently exists inside them.

For me, this is one of the places where Gestalt therapy, linguistics, and NLP unexpectedly meet. They ask different questions, use different vocabularies, but all three point toward the same observation: language carries far more information than its literal content alone.

Sometimes the most interesting part of a sentence isn't the idea it expresses. Sometimes I say: "Hmm... there is an interesting word there..." And I mean it.
