The map, in depth

Six ways the mind processes information

These are six functions the brain relies on to take in the world, make sense of it, and respond — the "Inside" half of the map. Most of what shows up as a diagnosis or a struggle traces back to one of these running differently than expected.

Read: Two Lenses, One Moment — how this map fits with Gestalt therapy →

1. Sensory-Perceptual Processing (Input)

Every second, your brain is flooded with raw signals — light hitting your retina, air vibrating your eardrum, pressure and temperature against your skin, quieter internal signals like your heartbeat or an ache in your stomach. None of that arrives pre-labeled. This system takes the flood and turns it into things you can actually use: a face instead of a pattern of light and shadow, a word instead of a string of sounds, "hungry" instead of a vague stomach sensation. Most of this happens instantly and outside your awareness — you don't experience raw data, you experience a finished picture, already assembled.

When this system struggles

Sensory sensitivity and overload — sounds, textures, or lights feeling unbearably intense — is a hallmark of autism spectrum experiences and shows up often in ADHD too. Difficulty reading the body's own internal signals (called interoception) is an active area of study in some anxiety disorders and eating disorders, where hunger, fullness, or emotional cues can feel hard to locate or trust.

2. Attentional Control & Arousal (Gating)

Think of this system as a spotlight paired with a bouncer. The bouncer decides what's allowed through — most of the sensory flood gets filtered out before you're even aware it existed, which is the only reason you're not overwhelmed by the hum of a refrigerator or the feel of your clothes right now. The spotlight can be aimed two ways: you can point it yourself, choosing to focus on this sentence — or something sudden can grab it from you, like your name spoken across a noisy room. Underneath both is a dial for overall alertness, running from foggy and half-asleep to sharp and wired.

When this system struggles

ADHD is fundamentally a story about this system — difficulty holding the spotlight where you want it, and a bouncer that lets too much noise through. Anxiety often shows the opposite distortion: the "grab your attention" half becomes hypersensitive, treating neutral things as urgent. Depression frequently dims the alertness dial itself, producing the fog and heaviness people describe as being unable to concentrate.

3. Memory & Learning (Storage & Retrieval)

Memory isn't one thing — it's at least three. There's the mental scratchpad you use to hold a phone number just long enough to dial it, gone in seconds unless you do something with it. There's the deeper archive of facts and lived experience — what you had for breakfast, what year the war ended — which starts fragile and slowly gets filed into permanent storage over time, often overnight during sleep. And there's a third, quieter kind that doesn't feel like "remembering" at all: the way your hands know how to type without thinking about each letter, or how a smell can trigger a feeling before you consciously recall why.

When this system struggles

Working-memory difficulty is one of the clearest features of ADHD — losing the thread mid-task, walking into a room and forgetting why. Depression commonly comes with real, measurable memory and concentration complaints, not just a feeling of it. PTSD affects memory in a distinctive way — the deeper, contextual memory of an event can under-encode while the emotional memory of it stays vivid and easily triggered, part of why flashbacks can feel so present-tense.

4. Executive Function (Control & Action Selection)

If the other systems gather information, this one decides what to do with it. It's the part of you that resists saying the blunt thing out loud, that holds a plan in mind across the many small decisions it takes to execute it, and that notices when a plan has stopped working and shifts strategy instead of stubbornly repeating it. Less a single decision-maker, more an ongoing negotiation between "what I want right now" and "what actually gets me where I'm going."

When this system struggles

ADHD prominently involves the inhibition side — impulses breaking through before they can be stopped. OCD involves the flexibility side in a specific, painful way: a thought or urge gets stuck, and the usual mechanism for shifting away from it doesn't disengage, so the mind — and often the body, through compulsions — loops. Depression often shows up here as difficulty with initiation specifically: the plan is clear, even wanting to do it is there, but starting feels like pushing against something heavy.

5. Affective & Social Cognition (Valuation)

Before you can decide what to do about something, you have to know how much it matters — that's this system's job. It tags experience with a rough emotional value: threat, reward, indifferent, urgent. A related piece of it is aimed outward, at other minds — inferring what someone else believes, wants, or feels, often from almost nothing: a tone of voice, a half-second facial expression, the pause before someone answers.

When this system struggles

In anxiety, the threat-detector runs too hot, tagging neutral situations as dangerous. In depression, the reward side often goes the other way — things that used to feel good stop registering at all, which is the clinical core of what's called anhedonia, not simply "low mood." Autism is associated with differences in the theory-of-mind side specifically — inferring others' mental states through a different, often more effortful route rather than an automatic one, a difference in processing style, not an absence of caring.

6. Motor Output & Language (Execution)

Everything upstream — perception, attention, memory, decision, emotional weighing — is invisible until this final system turns it into something the outside world can see or hear. That includes physical movement, but language is really a specialized case of the same problem: turning a wordless internal intention into a specific sequence of sounds or letters another mind can decode, in something close to real time.

When this system struggles

Depression often shows up here as visible slowing — movement, speech, even facial expression becoming noticeably less animated, sometimes called psychomotor slowing, a real physical symptom and not just a mood. Anxiety can do the opposite under acute stress — words not coming out right, the body feeling jittery or frozen. OCD compulsions are, structurally, this system running a repeated action to relieve internal distress, even when the person doing it fully recognizes the action isn't logically necessary.
