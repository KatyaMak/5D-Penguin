The Gestalt Approach

Understanding Gestalt therapy

Gestalt therapy is a humanistic, present-focused approach developed to help people become more aware of how they think, feel, and act — right now, in this moment — rather than mainly through analysis of the past. Below is a deeper look at the ideas that shape how I work.

◐

Here and Now

Rather than spending sessions reconstructing a detailed history, Gestalt therapy keeps returning attention to the present moment — what you notice in your body, what comes up as you speak, what happens between us in the room.

The idea is that the past shows up in the present anyway: in a tightened chest, a familiar defensiveness, a pattern of avoidance. By staying with what's happening now, we get direct access to it, rather than a secondhand report.

◑

Awareness

Awareness is the thread running through all of Gestalt work — simply noticing what's happening in your thoughts, feelings, and body as it happens, without immediately judging or fixing it.

It sounds simple, but most of us move through our days on autopilot. Building the habit of noticing — a tightened jaw, a held breath, a recurring thought — is often what makes everything else in therapy possible.

◔

Whole-Person Approach

Gestalt treats mind, body, and emotion as one connected system rather than separate channels. A clenched jaw, shallow breathing, or restless hands are read as part of the conversation, not background noise.

In session, I'll sometimes gently point to something physical I notice — not to interpret it for you, but so you can notice it yourself and see what it's connected to.

↻

Cycle of Contact

One way Gestalt therapy maps this: every need moves through a cycle, from a first sensation through awareness, action, and full contact, to rest — and at each stage, that cycle can get interrupted.

It's a genuinely useful map for noticing where things tend to get stuck for you.

◓

Unfinished Business

This refers to emotions from past experiences — grief, anger, longing — that were never fully felt or expressed, and that quietly keep shaping present-day reactions, often in situations that seem unrelated.

The goal isn't to relive the past for its own sake, but to let the emotional charge complete itself, so it stops leaking into the present.

◒

The Empty Chair

One of Gestalt's best-known techniques: speaking directly, out loud, to an empty chair representing a person, a part of yourself, or a feeling that isn't present — a parent, a younger version of you, even anxiety itself.

Voicing both sides of that conversation makes abstract, tangled feelings concrete. Clients often find things surface this way that stayed stuck for years in ordinary conversation about the situation.

◎

Experiment

Gestalt therapy doesn't rely only on talking about change — it invites you to try something, right now, in the room, and see what actually happens. That might be as quiet as noticing a sensation more closely, or as active as speaking a sentence you've never said out loud.

The point isn't performance or getting it "right" — it's finding out, through direct experience, what a shift actually feels like, rather than just discussing it in theory. Small, safe experiments like this are often where real movement happens.

◕

Paradoxical Theory of Change

It sounds counterintuitive: the Paradoxical Theory of Change holds that change happens not by trying hard to be different, but by fully becoming who you already are, right now — including the parts you'd rather change.

Pushing against yourself usually creates more of the very thing you're trying to escape. When you stop fighting a feeling or pattern and instead let yourself fully experience and understand it, it tends to loosen on its own — that shift, not willpower, is what Gestalt therapy is aiming for.

◈

Id, Ego & Personality

Gestalt uses these words differently than you might expect — not as fixed parts of a personality, but as functions the self moves through while making contact. Id is the raw, wordless layer: sensation, need, impulse, before it's been named. Ego is the part that chooses — what to reach for, what to say, what to do with what Id is sensing.

Personality is the story that forms afterward — the sense of "who I am" built up from those choices over time. None of these sit inside you as fixed structures; they're phases of one ongoing process, which is part of why Gestalt sees change as possible rather than as rewriting some permanent architecture.

◉

Self as Process

Gestalt doesn't treat the self as a fixed thing you have — it treats it as something you do, moment to moment, at the boundary where you meet the world. Self is less a noun and more a verb: it happens at contact, in the exchange between you and whatever — or whoever — you're meeting.

That's why the same person can show up differently in different relationships without that meaning something is wrong. The self is the current organization of Id, Ego, and Personality meeting a particular moment — not an empty shell, and not a fixed core underneath it all.

☍

Buber's Dialogue (I–Thou)

Philosopher Martin Buber described two fundamentally different ways of meeting another person: I–It, where the other becomes an object to manage, predict, or use — and I–Thou, where they're met as a whole being, as real and present as you are.

Most relationships move between both, and that's normal. But Gestalt therapy treats those rare I–Thou moments — full mutual presence, without an agenda — as some of the most healing things that can happen between two people, therapist and client included.

◫

Field Theory

Nothing happens in isolation. Field Theory holds that a thought, feeling, or behavior only makes sense in the context of everything surrounding it in that moment — your history, your relationships, the room you're sitting in, even what happened right before you walked in.

In practice, this means I'm not just listening to what you say — I'm paying attention to the whole situation you're describing it from. The same feeling can mean something different depending on the field it shows up in.

◐

Figure and Ground

At any moment, one thing pulls your attention forward — the figure — while everything else recedes into the background, the ground. A sound in a quiet room, a worry that won't let go, the thing you keep circling back to in conversation.

What becomes figure usually points to what needs attention most right now. Part of the work is noticing what keeps stepping forward for you, session after session — and what stays in the background, unspoken, until it doesn't.

◇

Phenomenology

Before Gestalt therapy is a technique, it's a way of paying attention: describing experience exactly as it shows up, before jumping to explain, diagnose, or interpret it. What did you actually notice — in your body, your thoughts, the room — before any story got added on top?

This means setting aside assumptions, including mine, about what something "should" mean. A tightened chest gets explored as a tightened chest first — the label, if there is one, can come later.
