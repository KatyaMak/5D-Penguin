The six functions and the cycle of contact

Gestalt describes experience moving through a cycle — sensation, awareness, mobilization, action, contact, withdrawal. Here's where each of the six cognitive functions actually does that work. A few genuinely show up at more than one point, so they're mapped that way rather than forced into one step apiece.

See the full cycle of contact →

1. Sensory-Perceptual Processing (Input)

Maps to: Sensation

This is where the cycle of contact actually begins. Before a need can be worked with, something has to register — a tightness in the chest, a sound in the room, a shift in someone's tone. In session, we often slow down here on purpose, asking "what are you noticing in your body right now" — not because it's the most important step, but because it's the one people skip fastest, especially if they learned early to override sensation as a habit of getting through the day.

2. Attentional Control & Arousal (Gating)

Maps to: Awareness (figure formation)

This is where a sensation becomes a figure — the Gestalt term for whatever rises into focus against the background of everything else happening. Not everything you sense becomes something you act on; this system decides what gets pulled forward and how much energy it's given. In therapy, this is often where we notice what a client keeps circling back to — or, just as tellingly, what keeps sliding into the background before it can be looked at directly.

3. Memory & Learning (Storage & Retrieval)

Maps to: Both ends — early Awareness, and closing Withdrawal/Assimilation

This system shows up twice. Early on, past learning quietly shapes what even counts as a figure — old patterns decide what feels urgent or unsafe before conscious thought gets involved. And at the very end, after real contact and resolution, this is what lets the experience be assimilated — folded into who you are, instead of left open. When a cycle doesn't complete, Gestalt calls what's left behind unfinished business — and it's this same system that keeps holding it, unresolved, often resurfacing in moments that seem to have nothing to do with the original one.

4. Executive Function (Control & Action Selection)

Maps to: Mobilization and Action

This is the system doing the work of gathering the energy a figure has earned and turning it into an actual next move. This is often the most delicate point to work with in the room: some people mobilize easily but the action never follows — energy that dissipates before it becomes movement — while others act too fast, skipping past awareness altogether. Either pattern is information about how this system typically resolves under pressure.

5. Affective & Social Cognition (Valuation)

Maps to: Awareness (why it matters) and Contact

This system does two jobs the cycle depends on. It's a large part of why something becomes a figure in the first place — emotional and relational weight is what makes a sensation worth attending to. And it's central to Contact itself, the moment of genuine meeting with another person that's really the heart of Gestalt work. Reading someone accurately, and letting yourself be read, is what turns proximity into actual contact.

6. Motor Output & Language (Execution)

Maps to: Action and Contact

This is the system that makes Action and Contact literal — the words actually said, the posture that actually shifts, the reaching that actually happens instead of staying internal. In session, this is often where an insight either becomes real or stays theoretical: someone can fully understand what they need and still not let the body or voice carry it into the room. What does or doesn't get physically expressed is often more informative than what gets said about it.
