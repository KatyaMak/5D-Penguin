Metacognitive Therapy

Metacognitive Therapy is an approach to mental well-being that helps you change your relationship with your mind by focusing on overall thinking habits. Instead of analyzing or questioning the details of each individual worry, it teaches you to notice loops of overthinking and rumination so you can let them pass by naturally, regaining control over your attention and finding relief from mental exhaustion.

The Cognitive Attentional Syndrome (CAS)

Most anxiety and depression aren't kept going by a single distressing thought - they're kept going by what happens after it: extended worry, rumination, scanning for threat, and trying to control or avoid the thought itself. Wells calls this pattern the Cognitive Attentional Syndrome, and it's the actual target of treatment, not the original thought.

Two people can have the exact same worry cross their mind. What decides whether it becomes a disorder is whether the CAS switches on and keeps running.

Metacognitive Beliefs

Underneath the CAS sit beliefs about thinking itself, not about the world. Some are positive - "worrying keeps me prepared," "if I analyze this enough I'll solve it." Others are negative - "I can't stop once I start," "this could make me lose control."

The positive ones give the CAS permission to start. The negative ones trap a person inside it, because trying to control something you believe is uncontrollable usually backfires - and then feels like proof the belief was right.

The S-REF Model

Self-Regulatory Executive Function is the model underneath all of this: an ongoing loop where attention, beliefs about one's own thinking, and long-term memory keep adjusting each other.

The CAS is what it looks like when that loop gets stuck in a self-reinforcing pattern - and metacognitive therapy exists to interrupt exactly that loop.

Detached Mindfulness

A thought passes through the mind constantly, unbidden. Detached Mindfulness is the practice of noticing it arrive without analyzing it, arguing with it, suppressing it, or following it anywhere.

It isn't relaxation and it isn't distraction. It's demonstrating, directly, that a thought can be left alone - which is the exact thing the negative metacognitive beliefs say can't be done.

Attention Training Technique (ATT)

A short daily exercise: attend to one sound among several, then shift rapidly between sounds, then hold several at once. There's no emotional content in it at all.

The point isn't the sounds. It's rehearsing flexible control over attention somewhere low-stakes, so that control is available later, in the moment a worry actually starts.

Worry and Rumination Postponement

When a worry starts, it gets noticed and set aside for a fixed slot later in the day - fifteen minutes, same time, every day - rather than argued with or pushed away on the spot.

This isn't suppression. It's a test: if the worry can be delayed, the belief that it can't be controlled was wrong from the start.

Situational Attentional Refocusing

In the middle of a triggering situation, attention gets redirected outward, onto what's actually there, instead of inward, onto how one is coming across or feeling.

Most anxiety keeps its own fuel supply going through self-monitoring. Interrupting that loop, in the moment, is often enough to change what happens next.

The Transdiagnostic Principle

Generalized anxiety, depression, PTSD, OCD, health anxiety - the content of the thought differs across all of them, but the CAS driving each one looks the same underneath.

That's why the same core techniques show up across very different presentations. Treatment doesn't need a different theory for every disorder, only the same one applied to whatever content shows up.

Process Over Content

MCT doesn't ask whether a thought is accurate, it is not trying to challenge or correct it. It asks a specific question: what is the person doing with a thought, and can that process itself change.

The thought is left alone. What shifts is the relationship to it - how long it gets engaged with, and how much control the person believes they have over it.
