Sensorimotor Psychotherapy

Sensorimotor Psychotherapy (SMT) is a body-based approach to the therapy process — the idea that difficult experiences live not just in memory, but in posture, breath, and the body's automatic responses. I bring it into sessions alongside Gestalt therapy, especially when a pattern seems to be showing up more in the body than in words.

Where it comes from

Somatic Experiencing

Trauma isn't the event itself, but an incomplete response to it. When a threat response gets interrupted before it completes, that activation can stay stored in the body.

Body-based trauma work

The body often holds what words can't easily reach — sensation, posture, and physical response can carry an experience even when it's hard to put into language.

Attachment patterns

Our early relationships shape how our nervous system learns to feel safe. Those patterns aren't fixed — they can shift with new relational experience, at any age.

How I might describe this

"Your nervous system learned patterns — how to stay safe, how to get needs met. Some formed early, others developed gradually. All of them made sense at the time. Right now, they might show up as tension, caution, or reactivity — in ways that feel familiar but hard to explain. What we're doing here is helping your body recognize that the context has changed. We work with sensation and movement, not just talk. Your nervous system is plastic — it can learn something new."

How patterns take hold in the body

A strategy that once kept you safe can become physical — held shoulders, a shallow breath, a jaw that never fully unclenches. And the posture itself keeps sending the same signal back to the nervous system, long after the original need has passed.

How this connects with Gestalt therapy

In Gestalt, the cycle of contact — awareness, mobilization, action, contact, withdrawal — needs to reach its endpoint for things to settle. In Sensorimotor work, an interrupted response — the fight or flight that never finished — needs to complete for the body to return to baseline. Two different vocabularies, describing something similar.

Gestalt gives us language for what's happening in your awareness right now. Sensorimotor gives us language for what your body is specifically doing in that moment. Together, they simply give us more to work with.

Gestalt

Awareness, the contact cycle, present-moment noticing, unfinished business.

Shared territory

Completion. Recognizing patterns. The moment something shifts. Embodied awareness.

Sensorimotor

Somatic language, tracking posture, movement experiments, working with the nervous system.

How we work with it

Tracking

I notice posture, breath, and small gestures, and gently name what I see: "I notice your shoulders come up when you speak about that."

Awareness

You become conscious of a pattern you've been living in without noticing. Noticing itself opens up a little space.

Experiment

A small movement is invited, never imposed — "What happens if your shoulders drop slightly?"

New feedback

A different posture sends a different signal to your nervous system: new configurations are possible, and it's safe.

Safety first

Building capacity to tolerate sensation, and a steady working relationship, before going any deeper.

Processing

Working with body memory directly — completing responses that got interrupted, at a pace the body can handle.

Integration

New patterns settling in — more range in how your body responds, holding up across everyday situations.

Along the way, a few things guide the pace: slowing down to notice sensation, moving attention between distress and a place of relative ease, working in small doses rather than all at once, and — when it's right — letting an interrupted impulse (to push away, to run, to curl in) finally complete, safely.

A small aside, for the curious: I'm also drawn to how the language we use for body sensations might someday inform mental-health data science — how therapy language gets understood computationally. It's a side interest, not part of the work we'd do together, but if it overlaps with your world, I'm always happy to talk shop.
