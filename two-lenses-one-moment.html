Two Lenses, One Moment

Gestalt therapy is built around direct experience — what's happening in you, right now, in the room, before anyone tries to explain it. It grew partly as a reaction against approaches that treated a person's inner life as a puzzle to be decoded from the outside, with the client's own present-moment awareness treated as secondary to the theory. And yet — here's a whole map of brain circuitry, sitting right next to it. Isn't that the exact move Gestalt therapy was built to resist?

Knowing the biological substrate of an experience doesn't explain the experience away. Understanding the physics of light — wavelengths, retinal cones, visual cortex — doesn't touch what it's actually like to see red. Both are true, complete descriptions of the same event, at different levels. Neither is more "real" than the other; they're just answering different questions. That's the relationship I want between the brain map and the therapy on this site: not one explaining away the other, but two vocabularies for the same territory, held side by side.

This isn't actually new territory for Gestalt work — it's an extension of something the field already does well. Working with somatic awareness — noticing tension in the shoulders, a held breath, the flutter before speaking — has always been part of this approach. It already treats the body as a legitimate source of information, not just the mind. What I'm adding is one more layer of that same noticing: not only "what am I sensing right now," but sometimes, where it's useful, "here's what's plausibly happening underneath that sensation." Offered as another way of paying attention — never as a correction of what you're actually feeling.

There's also a practical reason the map is organized the way it is: around six functions — how you take things in, how you focus, how you remember, how you decide, how you feel and read other people, how you act and speak — rather than around diagnostic labels. Diagnoses like anxiety, depression, ADHD, and OCD are useful for a lot of things, but they're really checklists of symptoms, and different people can meet the same checklist through very different underlying patterns. Organizing by function instead of by label isn't a new idea. It's close to a framework the National Institute of Mental Health built deliberately, called RDoC (Research Domain Criteria), specifically because researchers had concluded diagnostic categories don't map cleanly onto how the brain actually works. This site's six systems line up closely with that framework's core domains — the same six functions the brain relies on to take in the world, make sense of it, and respond, translated here into plain language.

So: two lenses, one moment. Sometimes knowing what's plausibly happening underneath an experience can make it feel a little less like something is wrong with you — and a little more like something your brain is doing, for understandable reasons, in a way that can shift.
